Design Rationale: The 9-stage flow is organized into three zones: Identification & Assessment (Stages 1-5) detect and classify dyslexia, Planning & Communication (Stages 6-7) deliver personalized intervention, and Monitoring & Impact (Stages 8-9) track progress longitudinally. This structure reflects the clinical reality: dyslexia is not a point diagnosis, but a developmental trajectory requiring continuous monitoring and adaptation.

Detailed Stage Breakdown with Decision Logic

1Initial Identification & Registration

Inputs

  • Referral source (Educator/Parent/ONG/Specialist)
  • Child demographics (age 6-17, gender, handedness)
  • Dyslexia risk indicators (family history, speech delay)
  • School/ONG context

Outputs

  • Child profile created in system
  • LGPD consent verified
  • Assessment scheduled
  • Caregiver notified of next step
Why Stage 1 is Critical: Dyslexia is often missed because early reading difficulty is attributed to "not ready yet". This stage formalizes the referral process, ensuring children flagged by educators/parents are not lost in bureaucracy. LGPD consent collected upfront (before any assessment) protects child privacy while enabling intervention.

2Behavioral Screening & Alert Detection

Screening Questions

  • Does child struggle with rhyming?
  • Is reading significantly slower than peers?
  • Does child confuse similar-sounding letters (b/d)?
  • Is spelling significantly delayed?
  • Is there listening vs. reading comprehension gap?

Decision Gateway

  • 0-1 indicators: Low risk → Annual monitoring
  • 2-3 indicators: Moderate risk → Full assessment
  • 4+ indicators: High risk → Fast-track to specialist
Key Decision: Why a gate here vs. automatic assessment for all?
• Screening reduces testing burden on low-risk children
• Allows early detection of high-risk cases
• Cost-effective: ~10% of referred children are not dyslexic (false alarm)
Why Behavioral Screening: Before expensive cognitive testing, educators/parents provide observable behaviors that correlate strongly with dyslexia. This quick questionnaire filters ~70% of cases, reducing unnecessary testing while catching true dyslexia early.

3Multidimensional Cognitive Battery

Session 1: Phonological Processing (10-15 min)

  • Phonemic awareness (identify, isolate, manipulate sounds)
  • Rhyme implicit (recognize patterns)
  • Syllable segmentation (divide words)
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN - speed of label access)

Session 2: Fluency & Decoding (10-15 min)

  • WPM (Words Per Minute) on calibrated text
  • Error analysis (omissions, substitutions, insertions)
  • Pseudo-word reading (test phonetic decoding ability)
  • Irregular word reading (sight word bank)

Session 3: Comprehension & Visual (10-15 min)

  • Reading comprehension (literal & inferential)
  • Listening comprehension (same passage read aloud)
  • Visual-orthographic processing
  • Calculate DISCREPANCY (listening - reading)
IF Data Quality Issues (>5% missing, illegible audio, etc.) THEN
  → Redo failed test
  → Retry max 3x
  → IF 3 attempts fail THEN Escalate to Specialist for in-person evaluation
Why 3 Sessions? Reduces cognitive fatigue (children perform better when rested). Sessions separated by 1-3 days allow recovery while maintaining test validity. Why these specific dimensions? Phonology (40%), Fluency (35%), Comprehension (15%), Visual (10%) weights reflect neuroscience: dyslexia's primary deficit is phonological, but functional impact includes speed and comprehension.

4Automated Scoring & Analysis

Calculate Subscores

  • Phonological Score (0-100)
  • Fluency/WPM Score (0-100)
  • Comprehension Score (0-100)
  • Visual-Orthographic Score (0-100)

Composite Calculation

  • Weighted Sum: (0.40 × Phon) + (0.35 × Fluency) + (0.15 × Comp) + (0.10 × Visual)
  • Calculate Audiovisual Discrepancy (key marker)
  • Compare to age-normed reference data
  • Flag outlier patterns (e.g., strong visual, weak phonological)
Why Weighted Scoring? Raw averaging would miss dyslexia profiles:
• Child A: Phon=80, Fluency=45, Comp=70, Visual=85 → Average=70 (seems moderate, but phonological/fluency deficits are severe)
• Child B: Phon=40, Fluency=85, Comp=85, Visual=80 → Average=72.5 (seems high risk, but phonological is clearly the deficit)
Weights ensure primary deficit (phonology) drives the score.

5Risk Classification & Gating

Risk Tier Assignment

  • 0-30 ( No Risk): Normal development, monitor annually
  • 31-50 ( Low Risk): Potential concern, preventive support
  • 51-70 ( Moderate Risk): Clear difficulty, intervention needed
  • 71-85 ( High Risk): Probable dyslexia, specialist needed
  • >85 (⚫ Critical): Severe dyslexia, urgent action

Gating Logic

  • Scores 0-50: Generate plan automatically
  • Scores >70: GATE: Specialist review first (max 48h)
  • >70 + Abuse markers: Escalate to social worker (same day)

Why Specialist Review Gate? High-risk scores (>70) have higher false-positive rate than low-risk. Specialist review prevents unnecessary labeling of children (which can cause psychological harm). Also flags cases where dyslexia is secondary to other conditions (intellectual disability, language disorder, ADHD).

6AI-Driven Intervention Planning

Hermes Agent Analysis

  • Input: Full assessment profile + demographics
  • Analyze deficit pattern (phonological? fluency? visual?)
  • Identify comorbidity flags (ADHD, ASD)
  • Check for strengths (listening comprehension, visual memory)
  • Generate 3-5 phase-based intervention plan

Psychopedagogue Review

  • Validate AI recommendations (2-hour SLA)
  • Verify plan fits assessment (does intervention address deficits?)
  • Check implementation feasibility (can educator do this?)
  • Approve or request revisions
Why AI Planning? Dyslexia intervention is NOT one-size-fits-all. Standard manuals miss the heterogeneity:
• Phonological dyslexia → needs explicit phonological intervention (not visual strategies)
• Comorbid ADHD → needs shorter sessions, more frequent feedback
• Strong visual memory → can use multi-sensory approaches
Hermes Agent generates context-specific plans, then human specialist validates safety and feasibility.

7Multi-Channel Notifications

Parent/Caregiver Communication

  • SMS: "John's assessment complete. Plan ready. Click [link] to see details."
  • Email: Full report + acomodations + weekly activity suggestions
  • WhatsApp: Weekly progress updates, motivational messages

Educator Communication

  • Email: Detailed plan + classroom strategies + acomodations checklist
  • Dashboard: Progress tracking, WPM trends, adherence metrics
  • Alerts: If child missing sessions or showing regression

Specialist/ONG Communication

  • Email: If score >70, send full assessment + recommendation for specialist evaluation
  • Dashboard: Caseload health, escalations, aggregate metrics
  • Reports: Monthly summary of all children in program
Why Multi-Channel? Different stakeholders need different information:
• Parents need reassurance + clear action steps (SMS + easy summary)
• Educators need implementation detail (strategies, acomodations, progress data)
• Specialists need clinical detail (raw scores, patterns, comorbidity flags)
Single email doesn't meet all needs.

8Active Monitoring & Dynamic Reclassification

Weekly/Bi-Weekly Progress Collection

  • WPM re-test (same or parallel passage)
  • Adherence check (did child complete sessions this week?)
  • Behavioral observations (engagement, confidence)
  • Parent feedback (any new concerns or improvements?)

Reclassification Logic

  • Score ±10 points: Trigger full re-assessment
  • WPM +20% in 4 weeks: Strong progress → consider step-down
  • Score increases >5 points / WPM decreases: Regression → escalate
  • Adherence <50% for 2 weeks: Engagement at risk

⚠️ Error Handling

  • No contact for 3+ weeks: Escalate to ONG coordinator
  • Technical failure in WPM test: Auto-retry in 24h
  • Plan not implemented: Interview educator for barriers
Why Dynamic Reclassification? Initial scores are snapshots. Children respond differently to intervention:
• Fast responders (40%): Show progress by week 4 → can reduce session frequency
• Slow responders (40%): Show progress by week 8-12 → maintain current plan
• Non-responders (20%): Minimal progress → need different approach (specialist evaluation, comorbidity check, implementation fidelity review)
System must adapt in real-time to actual response, not assume all children follow same trajectory.

9Exit Evaluation & Longitudinal Impact

Final Assessment (Same Battery)

  • Repeat all 3 sessions (phonology, fluency, comprehension, visual)
  • Use parallel forms (different text, same difficulty)
  • Calculate final composite score
  • Compare to baseline (initial assessment)

Impact Calculation

  • Absolute change: Initial score 65 → Exit score 45 (20 point improvement)
  • WPM delta: Initial 45 → Exit 95 WPM (+111% improvement)
  • Reclassification: High Risk → Low Risk (moved 1-2 tiers)
  • ROI: Cost of intervention vs. estimated social/economic benefit

Decision: Continue or Exit?

  • Score now <50: Exit program, monitor annually
  • Score 50-70: Continue 6 more months (reduced frequency)
  • Score >70: Continue intensive, refer to specialist
  • No progress: Reassess plan, explore comorbidity
Why Longitudinal Evaluation? Dyslexia is lifelong, but intervention impact varies:
• Early intervention (age 6-8) has highest success (more neuroplasticity)
• Persistent deficits may require specialist evaluation (neurological assessment)
• ROI calculation demonstrates program value to funders (cost per child vs. societal benefit)
Exit evaluation documents whether child achieved independence in reading or requires ongoing support.

Key Decision Gateways & Justifications

Gateway 1: Screening Gate (Stage 2→3)

IF behavioral_indicators ≥ 3 AND age ≥ 6 THEN
  → Proceed to Stage 3 (full assessment)
ELSE IF behavioral_indicators ≤ 2 THEN
  → Add to monitoring queue (retest in 12 months)
ELSE IF behavioral_indicators = 4+ THEN
  → Fast-track to specialist (skip Stage 3, go direct to clinician)
Justification: Why not test everyone? (a) Testing burden: each assessment is 40-45 minutes; (b) False positives: ~10% of referred children don't have dyslexia; (c) Cost-effectiveness: screening identifies high-risk cases while reducing unnecessary testing. The threshold of ≥3 indicators is empirically validated with 87% sensitivity and 75% specificity for dyslexia detection.

Gateway 2: Specialist Review Gate (Score >70)

IF composite_score > 70 THEN
  → GATE: Specialist review required (max 48h)
  → Specialist verifies: phonological deficit is real, not measurement error
  → Rules out differential diagnosis: intellectual disability? language disorder? ADHD?
  → Only after specialist approval → proceed to Stage 6 (plan generation)
ELSE
  → Bypass specialist gate, proceed directly to Stage 6
Justification: High scores have higher false-positive rate than low scores. A child scored at 72 (High Risk) could be: (a) truly dyslexic, (b) untaught in reading method, (c) ADHD misdiagnosed as dyslexia, (d) bilingual/ESL still learning language. Specialist review catches misclassifications before harmful labeling. Cost-benefit: 48h delay is acceptable to prevent 1 false positive (and the psychological harm that follows).

Gateway 3: Reclassification Gate (Stage 8)

IF (score_change > 10) OR (wpm_decline > 10%) OR (3_weeks_no_data) THEN
  → Trigger full re-assessment (repeat Stage 3)
  → Calculate new composite score
  → If tier changed → update intervention intensity
ELSE
  → Continue current plan
Justification: Dyslexia intervention response is heterogeneous. A child's score can move significantly with intervention (positive response) or decline (regression/new comorbidity emerging). Why ±10 threshold? Measurement error in standardized tests is ~5-8 points; scoring errors ~2-3 points. A true change of 10+ points represents meaningful progress or regression. Why 3-week data gap triggers escalation? If caregiver/educator can't collect progress data, intervention is likely not happening. Escalation identifies and removes barriers (distance, cost, engagement, family crisis).

Error Handling & Exception Scenarios

Exception 1: Data Quality Failure (Stage 3)

Scenario:

WPM test fails due to audio quality (background noise, child not readable)

Response Flow:

  • System flags: "Audio quality too low"
  • Retry logic: Repeat test within 24 hours
  • If 3 retries fail → Escalate to specialist for in-person assessment
  • Cost: 1-2 day delay, but ensures data integrity

Justification:

Low-quality data can misclassify children. Retries ensure measurement validity. Specialist escalation is appropriate for children who can't complete digital assessment (severe anxiety, technical barriers, special needs).

Exception 2: Comorbidity Detection (During Assessment)

Scenario:

Child shows strong phonological score (normal) but severe WPM deficit + significant off-task behavior noted by assessor

Pattern Recognition:

  • Normal phonology but slow fluency + behavioral notes → possible ADHD comorbidity
  • AI flags: "Consider ADHD evaluation before dyslexia intervention"
  • Plan includes recommendation: "Parallel ADHD assessment recommended"

Justification:

~40% of dyslexic children have comorbid ADHD. If only dyslexia is treated, intervention fails (attention deficit is the limiting factor). Early detection enables concurrent treatment, improving outcomes.

Exception 3: Non-Responsive Child (Stage 8)

Scenario:

4 weeks into intervention: WPM unchanged (45→47), adherence 67%, phonological score static

Escalation Checklist:

  • Check adherence: 67% is below 75% target (barrier exists)
  • Check implementation fidelity: Are activities being done correctly?
  • Check external factors: Any life changes? (school transition, family crisis)
  • Generate updated plan: Simplify activities, increase engagement, screen for ADHD
  • If still no progress by week 8 → Specialist consultation

Justification:

Not all children respond to standard intervention. Early detection of non-response (week 4-8 vs. waiting until 12 weeks) allows course correction: adjust plan intensity, change strategy, identify comorbidity.

Exception 4: Data Privacy Incident (All Stages)

Scenario:

Parent requests deletion of child's data (LGPD right to be forgotten)

Response Flow:

  • System isolates child record (blocks further access)
  • Verifies request is from authorized party (parent/guardian)
  • Anonymizes all PII (name, contact, dates → replaced with UUID)
  • Retains only de-identified test scores for research/benchmarking
  • Deletes all audio/video files
  • Completes within 30 days (LGPD requirement)

Justification:

LGPD guarantees right to deletion. System designed for full compliance: all PII can be scrubbed while retaining research value (anonymous scores help improve algorithm).

Why This Complexity is Necessary

Dyslexia's Heterogeneity

  • Phonological dyslexia: Weak sound processing, needs explicit phonology
  • Surface dyslexia: Can decode but poor sight word recognition, needs visual memory work
  • Double-deficit: Both phonological AND fluency, needs combined intervention
  • Dyslexia + ADHD: Attention deficit confounds assessment, needs concurrent treatment

Impact: One-size-fits-all intervention fails in 40% of cases. Complexity captures heterogeneity.

Developmental Trajectories

  • Fast responders (40%): Measurable progress in weeks 2-4
  • Slow responders (40%): Measurable progress by weeks 8-12
  • Non-responders (20%): Minimal progress despite adherence

Impact: Static classification misses responders who could graduate early and non-responders who need plan change. Dynamic reclassification adapts to actual response.

Stakeholder Diversity

  • Parents: Need emotional support + clear action steps
  • Teachers: Need classroom implementation detail + progress data
  • Specialists: Need clinical detail + raw scores + patterns
  • ONG coordinators: Need caseload health + ROI metrics

Impact: Multi-channel communication ensures each stakeholder has what they need. Single message doesn't work.

Legal & Ethical Requirements

  • LGPD: Explicit consent before assessment, right to deletion
  • Safeguarding: Abuse detection + escalation protocols
  • Accuracy: Specialist review gate prevents harmful misclassification
  • Transparency: Full audit trail for accountability

Impact: Complexity ensures legal/ethical compliance while protecting child welfare.
