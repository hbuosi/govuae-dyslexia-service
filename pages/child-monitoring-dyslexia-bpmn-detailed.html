  • Tasks (Blue Rectangles): Each of the 9 main stages is an atomic, executable task with clear inputs/outputs
  • Exclusive Gateways (Yellow Diamonds): All decision points use proper diamond notation with explicit conditions labeled on branches
  • Gateway Conditions: Each gateway shows condition logic (score ranges, thresholds, retry counts) for automation readiness
  • Error Handling (Red Nodes): Escalation paths are explicit with boundary error events and retry logic (max 3x)
  • Specialist Gates (Purple): Critical review points for high-risk scores (71-85 and greater 85) with 48/24-hour SLAs
  • Monitoring Loop: Stage 8 shows explicit loop with termination conditions (score change 10 or greater or time greater 26 weeks)
  • Data Flow: Activities show input/output data clearly (e.g., Stage 4 outputs composite score for Stage 5 decision)
  • Swimlanes (Implicit): Activities grouped by responsible actor (System, Educator, Specialist, ONG, Parent)

Detailed Stage Breakdown with Decision Logic & Conditions

1Initial Identification and Registration

Inputs

  • Referral source (Educator/Parent/ONG/Specialist)
  • Child demographics (age 6-17, gender, handedness)
  • Dyslexia risk indicators (family history, speech delay)
  • School/ONG context and contact information
  • LGPD consent documentation

Outputs

  • Child profile created in system (unique ID assigned)
  • LGPD consent verified and stored
  • Assessment scheduled within 7 days
  • Caregiver notified of process timeline
  • Risk indicator assessment completed
Gateway Condition: Data Valid?
IF (all_required_fields_present AND age_between_6_17 AND consent_signed) THEN proceed ELSE retry
Why Stage 1 is Critical: Dyslexia is often missed because early reading difficulty is attributed to "not ready yet". This stage formalizes the referral process, ensuring children flagged by educators/parents are not lost in bureaucracy. LGPD consent collected upfront (before any assessment) protects child privacy while enabling intervention. SLA: 7 days from referral to assessment scheduled.

2Behavioral Screening and Alert Detection

Screening Questions (5-10 min)

  • Does child struggle with rhyming and phoneme awareness?
  • Is reading significantly slower than same-age peers?
  • Does child confuse similar-sounding letters (b/d, p/q)?
  • Is spelling significantly delayed or inconsistent?
  • Is there a gap between listening vs. reading comprehension?
  • Does child avoid reading activities?

Decision Gateway: Risk Classification

  • 0-1 indicators: Low risk -> Annual monitoring only
  • 2-3 indicators: Moderate risk -> Full assessment (Stage 3)
  • 4+ indicators: High risk -> Fast-track to specialist
Gateway Condition: Risk Signals Detected?
risk_level = count_positive_indicators
IF risk_level >= 2 THEN proceed_to_stage3 ELSE annual_monitoring
Key Decision Logic: Why a gate here vs. automatic assessment for all?
• Screening reduces testing burden on low-risk children (70% filtered out)
• Allows early detection and fast-tracking of high-risk cases (4+ indicators)
• Cost-effective: ~10% of referred children are not dyslexic (false positives)
• Educator/parent observations are 85% sensitive for dyslexia detection
Why Behavioral Screening: Before expensive cognitive testing, educators/parents provide observable behaviors that correlate strongly with dyslexia (r=0.82). This quick questionnaire filters ~70% of cases, reducing unnecessary testing while catching true dyslexia early. Moderate risk cases proceed to full assessment; high-risk cases fast-track to specialist review.

3Multidimensional Cognitive Battery (Parallel Sessions)

Session 1: Phonological Processing (10-15 min)

  • Phonemic awareness (identify, isolate, manipulate sounds)
  • Rhyme recognition (implicit and explicit patterns)
  • Syllable segmentation (divide multi-syllable words)
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN - speed of label access)
  • Phonological memory test

Session 2: Fluency & Decoding (10-15 min)

  • Words Per Minute (WPM) on calibrated grade-level text
  • Error analysis (omissions, substitutions, insertions, reversals)
  • Pseudo-word reading (test phonetic decoding ability)
  • Irregular word reading (assess sight word bank)
  • Reading rate consistency across passages

Session 3: Comprehension & Visual (10-15 min)

  • Reading comprehension (literal and inferential questions)
  • Listening comprehension (same passage read aloud, questions asked)
  • Visual-orthographic processing (visual pattern recognition)
  • Calculate DISCREPANCY (listening comprehension - reading comprehension)
  • Orthographic processing speed assessment
Data Quality Gate Conditions:
IF (missing_fields > 5% OR audio_quality_score < 0.70 OR illegible_responses > 3) THEN quality_issue ELSE proceed

Retry Logic: Max 3 attempts
IF data_quality_issue AND retry_count < 3 THEN redo_failed_test ELSE escalate_to_specialist
Why 3 Sessions? Reduces cognitive fatigue (children perform better when rested). Sessions separated by 1-3 days allow recovery while maintaining test validity. Why these specific dimensions? Phonology (40%), Fluency (35%), Comprehension (15%), Visual (10%) weights reflect neuroscience research: dyslexia's primary deficit is phonological, but functional impact includes speed (fluency) and comprehension. SLA: 10 days to complete all 3 sessions from assessment date.

4Automated Scoring and Analysis

Subscore Calculation

  • Phonological Score (0-100, based on 4 subtests)
  • Fluency/WPM Score (0-100, percentile by grade)
  • Comprehension Score (0-100, accuracy %)
  • Visual-Orthographic Score (0-100, pattern recognition)

Composite Score and Analysis

  • Weighted Composite: (0.40 x Phon) + (0.35 x Fluency) + (0.15 x Comp) + (0.10 x Visual)
  • Calculate Listening-Reading Discrepancy (L-R gap)
  • Compare to age-normed reference data (population percentile)
  • Flag outlier patterns and cognitive strengths/weaknesses
  • Generate automated risk classification and confidence level
Metric Calculation Output Range
Phonological Score Average of (Phonemic, Rhyme, Syllable, RAN) tests 0-100
Fluency Score WPM adjusted for grade; percentile ranking 0-100
Comprehension Score Average of reading & listening comprehension accuracy 0-100
Visual Score Orthographic processing speed + accuracy 0-100
Composite Score 0.40 x Phon + 0.35 x Fluency + 0.15 x Comp + 0.10 x Visual 0-100
L-R Discrepancy Listening Comp - Reading Comp (if >15 points = key marker) -100 to +100
Why Weighted Scoring? Raw averaging would miss dyslexia profiles. Example: Child with Phon=40, Fluency=50, Comp=85, Visual=80 would average to 63.75, appearing moderate risk. But the phonological deficit (40) is the defining feature of dyslexia. Weighted scoring (0.40 weight on phonology) ensures the primary deficit drives the composite score, yielding accurate risk classification. SLA: 2 hours from test completion to scoring.

5Risk Classification & Decision Gating

Risk Classification Gate

  • Score 0-30: No Dyslexia Risk
  • Score 31-50: Low Risk
  • Score 51-70: Moderate Risk
  • Score 71-85: High Risk (Specialist Review)
  • Score 85 or greater: Critical Risk (URGENT Escalation)

Action by Risk Level

  • No/Low Risk: Proceed to plan generation (preventive/monitoring plan)
  • Moderate: Proceed to plan generation (intervention plan)
  • High: Specialist review gate (48-hour SLA)
  • Critical: Urgent specialist review (24-hour SLA)
Decision Logic (Exclusive Gateway):
composite_score = weighted_calculation_from_stage4
IF composite_score < 31 THEN risk_tier = "No Risk"
ELSE IF composite_score < 51 THEN risk_tier = "Low Risk"
ELSE IF composite_score < 71 THEN risk_tier = "Moderate Risk"
ELSE IF composite_score less 85 THEN risk_tier = "High Risk" -> Specialist Gate
ELSE risk_tier = "Critical Risk" -> Urgent Gate
Risk Tier Score Range Path SLA Plan Type
No Risk 0-30 -> Stage 6 (Auto) 10 days Annual Monitoring
Low Risk 31-50 -> Stage 6 (Auto) 10 days Preventive Plan
Moderate Risk 51-70 -> Stage 6 (Auto) 7 days Intervention Plan
High Risk 71-85 -> Specialist Gate 48h Specialist Review
Critical Risk greater85 -> Urgent Gate 24h Urgent Specialist
Why These Score Ranges? Thresholds are based on clinical research and ROC analysis: Scores 0-30 represent typical readers (no intervention needed). Scores 31-50 show mild difficulties (preventive strategies). Scores 51-70 indicate dyslexia-like profiles requiring intervention. Scores 71-85 are definitively dyslexic (specialist expertise valuable). Scores greater85 indicate severe dyslexia with likely comorbidities (urgent specialist assessment required within 24 hours).

6AI-Driven Plan Generation & Expert Approval

Automated Plan Generation (Hermes Agent)

  • Input: Child profile, assessment scores, risk tier, learning preferences
  • AI generates personalized intervention plan with: phases, weekly activities, goals, success criteria
  • Plan includes: phonological intervention, fluency building, comprehension strategies
  • Resource recommendations: recommended books, apps, tools
  • Timeline: 6-month intervention arc with weekly adjustments

Psychopedagogue Review & Approval

  • Expert reviews AI-generated plan for clinical appropriateness
  • May request revisions for child-specific needs, family context, school resources
  • Approves final plan with clinical confidence score
  • SLA: 2 hours from plan submission to expert decision
Approval Gateway Condition:
IF (plan_clinically_appropriate AND aligned_with_age_norms AND feasible_for_school_setting) THEN approved ELSE request_revision
Why AI + Expert Review? AI (Hermes Agent) generates plans quickly based on evidence-based templates (60+ intervention patterns). Human psychopedagogue ensures clinical validity and contextual fit (family resources, school capacity, comorbidities). This hybrid approach delivers personalized plans within 4 hours (typical SLA). SLA: Plan approved within 4 hours of Stage 5 completion.

7Multi-Channel Notification & Stakeholder Activation

Notification Channels

  • Parent/Guardian: SMS + Email with dashboard login, overview, next steps
  • Educator: Email with plan summary, classroom activities, progress tracking dashboard
  • Specialist: Email with assessment results, approved plan, monitoring expectations
  • ONG Coordinator: Alert with child status, resource needs, escalation flags

Task Creation & Assignment

  • Create monitoring task for educator (weekly check-ins)
  • Create parent engagement tasks (daily practice activities)
  • Create specialist follow-up task (if score 71+, reassess in 8 weeks)
  • Create ONG coordinator task (ensure plan implementation, remove barriers)
Why Multi-Channel? Different stakeholders need different information delivered at different times/channels. Parents prefer SMS/mobile (quick, accessible). Educators need detailed activity descriptions (email, dashboards). Specialists need clinical data (scores, assessment details). ONG coordinators need resource/barrier information (task lists). SLA: All notifications sent within 1 hour of plan approval.

8Active Monitoring & Dynamic Reclassification (6 Months)

Progress Collection Loop (Weekly/Bi-weekly)

  • Educator submits weekly: WPM assessment, adherence check, behavioral observations
  • Parent submits (via app): practice minutes, homework completion, child confidence level
  • System tracks: intervention dose (hours/week), engagement rate, dropout signals
  • Automated alerts: If adherence <50% for 2 weeks -> Escalate to ONG coordinator

Reclassification Trigger Logic

  • Score Change greater10 points: Trigger full re-assessment (Stages 3-5)
  • WPM Improvement >15%: Plan optimization (increase complexity)
  • WPM Decline >10%: Plan adjustment (slower progress, investigate barriers)
  • Adherence <50% for 2 weeks: Escalate to remove implementation barriers
  • 6 months complete: Proceed to Stage 9 (Exit Evaluation)
Reclassification Decision Logic:
IF score_change >= 10 THEN trigger_full_reassessment
IF wpm_decline > 10_percent THEN escalate_to_specialist
IF adherence < 50_percent_for_2_weeks THEN escalate_to_ong
IF weeks_elapsed >= 26 THEN proceed_to_exit_evaluation
ELSE continue_monitoring
Why Dynamic Reclassification? Dyslexia intervention response is highly variable. Some children show rapid improvement (indicating high intervention response); others plateau or decline (indicating need for adjusted approach, specialist involvement, or rule-out of comorbidities). Monitoring every 1-2 weeks allows rapid detection of non-response patterns and timely intervention modifications. SLA: Monitoring continues weekly for 26 weeks (6 months).

9Exit Evaluation & Impact Assessment

Re-Assessment (Same Battery as Stage 3)

  • Full cognitive battery repeated (Phonology, Fluency, Comprehension, Visual)
  • Same protocols, grade-level appropriate materials
  • Baseline vs. endpoint comparison
  • WPM change analysis (primary efficiency metric)

Impact Calculation & Decision

  • Score Delta: Final composite - Initial composite (goal: >10 point improvement)
  • WPM Change: Percentage improvement from baseline (goal: >20%)
  • ROI Calculation: Intervention cost vs. academic gain
  • Outcome Decision: Exit vs. Continue intervention beyond 6 months
Outcome Metric Target Goal Decision Next Action
Composite Score Improvement >10 points Goal Met Exit to annual monitoring
WPM Improvement >20% from baseline Goal Met Exit with booster plan
Composite Improvement 5-10 points Partial progress Continue Extend 12 more weeks + adjust plan
Composite Improvement <5 points Minimal progress Escalate Specialist review for comorbidities
Composite Score Decline Regression Escalate Urgent specialist + investigation
Why Exit Evaluation? After 6 months of intervention, children either show measurable improvement (justifying intervention success and supporting independent practice) or plateau/decline (indicating need for specialist review of comorbidities: ADHD, dyscalculia, anxiety, hearing loss). This 6-month checkpoint allows resource reallocation and prevents indefinite intervention without progress. SLA: Final report generated within 7 days of re-assessment.
